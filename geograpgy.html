Geographical Diversification of West Bengal

1. The Himalayan Region (North Bengal)

The northern part of West Bengal is dominated by the Eastern Himalayas. The Darjeeling hills and Kalimpong regions are known for their scenic mountains, tea gardens, and forests. The highest peak in the region is Sandakphu (3,636 meters). The Teesta River is a major river that flows through this region.

2. The Terai and Dooars

Just below the Himalayas, the Terai and Dooars region is characterized by dense forests, grasslands, and tea plantations. This area is home to rich biodiversity and famous national parks like Jaldapara and Gorumara, where one can find Indian one-horned rhinoceroses and elephants.

3. The Gangetic Plains (Central Bengal)

Central West Bengal consists of the fertile Gangetic plains, where rivers like the Ganges, Hooghly, and Damodar play a crucial role in agriculture. This region is densely populated and includes major cities like Kolkata, Bardhaman, and Murshidabad. The landscape is marked by floodplains, fertile alluvial soil, and wetlands.

4. The Western Plateau

The western part of the state, including districts like Purulia, Bankura, and West Midnapore, is part of the Chotanagpur Plateau. This area is known for its laterite soil, undulating hills, and rich mineral resources. The Ajodhya Hills and forests of this region support tribal communities and wildlife.

5. The Coastal and Sundarbans Region (South Bengal)

The southernmost part of West Bengal is home to the world's largest mangrove forest, the Sundarbans. This region is shaped by the confluence of the Ganges, Brahmaputra, and Meghna rivers, forming a vast delta. The Sundarbans is famous for its unique ecosystem, home to the Royal Bengal Tiger and various marine species. The coastal belt of Digha and Mandarmani is known for its beaches and fishing activities.
